Going solar sounds simple enough. Pick a system, hire an installer, and start saving on your electricity bill. But before a single panel goes up, there's a step that catches a lot of homeowners (and some installers) off guard: getting your permits.

Solar permits aren't just bureaucratic red tape. They're how local governments make sure your system is safe, your roof can handle the load, and your electrical work won't cause problems down the line. Skip them, and you risk fines, failed inspections, or complications when you sell your home.

This guide covers what solar permits are, why they're required, what they cost, and what the process actually looks like from start to finish.

> **Key Takeaways**
>
> - A solar permit is an official approval from your local AHJ that authorizes installation of a solar energy system. Almost every grid-tied system in the U.S. requires one.
> - Most installations require both a building permit and an electrical permit, plus a utility interconnection agreement (PTO) before the system can go live.
> - Permit fees typically run $100 to $500. Total permitting timelines range from a few weeks (residential) to several months (commercial).
> - Requirements vary significantly by state, county, and city. There's no national standard, which makes local knowledge valuable.
> - Shovels tracks solar permit data across 1,800+ jurisdictions, giving you visibility into market activity, contractor performance, and AHJ timelines.

## What Is a Solar Permit?

A solar permit is an official approval from your local <a href="https://www.shovels.ai/blog/ahj-in-construction/" target="_blank"><strong>Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ)</strong></a>, usually a city or county building department, that authorizes you to install a solar energy system on your property.

Think of it as a green light from your local government. Before your installer can start work, the AHJ reviews the plans for your system and confirms they meet local building codes, electrical standards, and zoning requirements. After installation, an inspector comes out to verify the work was done correctly.

In most cases, your solar installer pulls these permits on your behalf. But it's worth understanding what's happening and why.

## Do You Need a Permit to Install Solar Panels?

Almost certainly yes. In the vast majority of U.S. jurisdictions, a permit is required before you can legally install a solar energy system.

The only real exceptions are very small off-grid systems (think: a single panel powering a shed) in jurisdictions that explicitly waive the requirement. For anything grid-tied, or anything above a few hundred watts, assume a permit is required.

Some states have worked to streamline the process. California, for instance, requires jurisdictions to offer an expedited permit review for small residential solar systems under SB 379. But streamlined doesn't mean skipped. The permit still needs to happen.

## What Permits Do You Need to Install Solar Panels?

The full list depends on your jurisdiction, but most solar installations require some combination of the following:

**Building permit** — Covers the structural side of the installation: roof penetrations, mounting hardware, and panel placement. The AHJ wants to confirm your roof can hold the weight and the installation won't compromise the building.

**Electrical permit** — Covers the wiring, inverters, disconnects, and connection to your home's electrical panel. This is often reviewed separately by the AHJ's electrical inspector.

**Interconnection agreement** — This one comes from your utility company, not the AHJ. Before your system can feed power back to the grid, the utility has to approve the connection. The final step is called a **Permission to Operate (PTO)**.

**HOA approval** — Not technically a permit, but if you live in a community with a homeowners association, you may need their sign-off alongside the permit process. Many states now limit HOAs from blocking solar installations outright, but the approval process can still add time.

## The Solar Permit Process, Step by Step

Here's what the typical solar permit process looks like, from signing a contract to actually turning the system on:

1. **Hire a licensed solar installer.** A licensed installer handles the permitting in most cases. Verify they're familiar with your local AHJ's specific requirements.
2. **System design and plan set.** The installer creates a detailed plan set: panel layout, electrical diagrams, structural calculations.
3. **Submit the permit application to your AHJ.** The application, plan set, and any required fees go to your local building department.
4. **Plan review.** The AHJ reviews the plans against local codes. This can take a few days or a few weeks depending on the jurisdiction.
5. **Permit issued.** Once approved, the permit is issued and installation can begin.
6. **Installation.** Your installer puts up the panels and completes the electrical work.
7. **Inspection.** An AHJ inspector comes out to verify the installation matches the approved plans and meets code.
8. **Interconnection and PTO.** After passing inspection, your installer submits interconnection paperwork to the utility. Once the utility approves, you receive Permission to Operate and can turn the system on.

That last step is worth flagging. Even after a clean inspection, you can't legally run a grid-tied system until the utility issues PTO. PTO timelines vary by utility and can range from a few days to several weeks.

## How Long Does Solar Permitting Take?

For residential systems, the permit process can take anywhere from same-day approval to **eight weeks**. Commercial projects take longer, often two to six months.

We ran a quick query in the Shovels database and found that in the past 12 months, 62% of residential solar permits obtained same-day approval. About 90% were issued within 30 days.

That said, there are variables that slow things down:

- **AHJ backlog** — Some jurisdictions are overwhelmed with permit applications and simply take longer to review.
- **Incomplete applications** — Missing documents or design errors kick the application back and restart the review clock.
- **Plan revisions** — If the AHJ flags issues with the plan set, the installer has to revise and resubmit.
- **Utility interconnection queue** — After passing inspection, you're in your utility's queue for PTO. Some utilities move faster than others.

Jurisdictions that have moved to online permitting or offer <a href="https://www.shovels.ai/blog/over-the-counter-permits/" target="_blank">over-the-counter review</a> for small residential systems tend to be faster. If turnaround time matters to your project, it's worth asking your installer what the local track record looks like before you sign anything.

If delays are a recurring issue, or you're managing installations across multiple jurisdictions, it may be worth working with a <a href="https://www.shovels.ai/blog/permit-expediter/" target="_blank">permit expediter</a>. They know the local AHJ processes, have relationships with plan reviewers, and can often route applications through faster tracks that a first-time applicant wouldn't know to look for.

## Solar Permits Vary Significantly by State (and County, and City)

There's no national solar permitting standard. Every state, county, and municipality can have its own process, requirements, and fee structure. This creates a fragmented landscape that's genuinely frustrating for installers working across multiple markets.

A few examples of how much this varies:

- **California** requires jurisdictions to offer an expedited, low-cost permit process for small residential systems, and has some of the highest solar permit volume in the country.
- **Florida** has state-level preemption that limits local governments from blocking solar installations outright.
- **Texas** has no statewide solar permitting requirements, which means rules vary dramatically from city to city.

## How Much Does a Solar Permit Cost?

Permit fees for residential solar installations typically run between **$100 and $500**, though the range is wide. Some jurisdictions charge a flat fee, while others calculate it based on system size or project value. According to Shovels data from the past 12 months, the median cost of a residential solar permit is about $352.

In the context of total installation cost (averaging around $15,000 to $25,000 for a typical home before incentives), permit fees are a small line item. But fee structures vary enough across jurisdictions that they can affect where solar development activity concentrates, which matters a lot if you're an installer or equipment supplier trying to prioritize markets.

A few states have capped or eliminated solar permit fees to encourage adoption. Others haven't updated their fee schedules in years, creating inconsistency even across neighboring cities.

## When Is the Best Time to Install Solar Panels (and Pull the Permit)?

From a practical standpoint, **spring and early summer** tend to be the best time to start the permitting process. AHJ offices are less backlogged than they are at year-end, installers have more availability before peak summer demand kicks in, and you'll start generating power during the highest-sunlight months.

More importantly: watch federal and state incentive deadlines. The federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC) is currently at 30% for residential systems. If there's any policy uncertainty on the horizon, sooner tends to be better for locking in current incentive levels.

If you're buying a home with solar already installed, or planning a solar installation as part of a larger renovation, permit timing should be factored into the project schedule from day one, not treated as an afterthought.
