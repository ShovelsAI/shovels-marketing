Permit delays are one of the most common ways construction projects go over budget. A **permit expediter** exists specifically to prevent that. They help navigate the municipal approval process so contractors, developers, and project managers don't have to.

## What Is a Permit Expediter?

A **permit expediter** is a trained professional who manages the building permit application process on behalf of a client, from initial research to final approval. Their job is to get permits approved faster and with fewer setbacks than the average applicant managing on their own.

Experienced permit expediters typically have backgrounds in construction, planning, architecture, or real estate, and have spent years building direct relationships with building department staff and plan reviewers across specific jurisdictions. That combination of process knowledge and local relationships is what makes permit expediting services valuable.

The term is sometimes used interchangeably with "permit runner" or "permit expeditor" (alternate spelling). In practice, a permit runner often refers to someone focused purely on submitting paperwork, while a permit expediter takes a broader strategic role throughout the full approval process.

## Why Building Permit Expediting Matters

Getting a permit approved is a necessity for every trade on a construction schedule. When permit review stalls, so does every downstream task: subcontractor scheduling, material deliveries, project financing, and in commercial projects, lease start dates and revenue.

**Building permit expediting** matters because:

- Permit processing times vary enormously by jurisdiction, from a few days to several months
- Application errors or missing documentation can send a submission back for a full recheck cycle
- Each jurisdiction's <a href="https://www.shovels.ai/blog/ahj-in-construction/" target="_blank">Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ)</a> sets its own requirements, workflows, and unwritten norms that insiders know and outsiders don't
- For multi-site rollouts, managing permits across dozens of jurisdictions simultaneously is practically impossible without dedicated expertise

For commercial projects especially, the cost of delays typically far exceeds the cost of professional permit expediting services.

## What Does a Permit Expediter Do?

A building permit expediter's day-to-day work spans research, documentation, coordination, and follow-through. Their core services include:

- **Due diligence:** Researching zoning laws, building codes, and jurisdiction-specific permit requirements before anything is submitted
- **Plan review:** Checking construction drawings against local code requirements to catch issues before the application is submitted
- **Application preparation:** Assembling and submitting the complete permit package correctly the first time
- **Coordination:** Communicating directly with plan reviewers, building officials, and city staff throughout the review process
- **Status tracking:** Following up on pending applications and managing any recheck cycles or corrections
- **OTC review coordination:** Experienced expediters often arrange <a href="https://www.shovels.ai/blog/over-the-counter-permits/" target="_blank">over-the-counter permit reviews</a> for qualifying scopes, getting plans reviewed on the spot rather than entering the standard queue

That last point matters: a skilled permit expediter doesn't just wait in the review queue. They actively look for ways to route applications through faster tracks when the project scope qualifies.

## When Should You Hire a Permit Expediter?

Not every project needs one. Simple, straightforward work in a familiar jurisdiction is often manageable without a construction permit expediter. But the math shifts quickly as project complexity increases.

Hiring a permit expediter makes sense when:

- You're working in an unfamiliar jurisdiction with processes you don't know
- The project has a tight timeline where any permit delay creates downstream costs
- You're managing a multi-site rollout across different cities or counties
- Previous submissions have been rejected and you're cycling through rechecks
- Your team doesn't have bandwidth to track and manage the application in-house
- The project involves complex permits: commercial tenant improvements, mixed-use, food service, or anything requiring multiple concurrent approvals

The most common clients for permit expediting services are construction project managers, architects, directors of entitlements, tenant coordinators, and retail development teams managing rollouts at scale.

## How Much Does a Permit Expediter Cost?

Permit expediter costs typically range from $500 to $15,000+ for most projects, according to <a href="https://permitplace.com/permit-expediter-cost-guide/" target="_blank">Permit Place</a>. Residential projects tend toward the lower end ($500–$2,500), while commercial single-site work commonly runs $1,500 and up. Complex commercial projects, multi-jurisdictional work, or high-difficulty markets can push costs significantly higher.

Common pricing structures include:

- **Flat fee per permit:** Most common for defined-scope projects
- **Hourly rate:** Ranges from roughly $50–$75/hour for basic permit running, more for complex expediting
- **Per-counter-visit fee:** Used by some expediters for simpler jurisdictions
- **Monthly retainer:** Used by development teams with ongoing high-volume permit needs

Note that expediter fees are separate from the permit fees paid directly to the building department. Those are set by the jurisdiction and aren't negotiable.

The practical way to think about cost: a permit expediter's fee is an investment against delays. In commercial construction, a single week of schedule slip can cost more in carrying costs, trade rescheduling, and lost revenue than the entire expediting fee.
