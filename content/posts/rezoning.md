Rezoning is one of those processes that sounds straightforward until you're actually in it. A property owner wants to change how their land can be used (for example, converting farmland to residential, or a residential lot to mixed-use), but discovers that what's allowed on a parcel isn’t up to them. It's a local government decision, and changing it takes time, money, and community buy-in.

Whether you're a developer evaluating a site, a landowner exploring options, or an investor underwriting a deal that depends on a zoning change, here's what you need to know.

> **Key Takeaways**
>
> - Rezoning is the process of changing a property's official land-use designation, which determines what can legally be built or operated there
> - It requires formal approval from local government, typically involving a planning commission and a city council or county board vote
> - The process can take anywhere from a few months to several years depending on jurisdiction, complexity, and community opposition
> - Rezoning generally does increase property value, but the magnitude depends on the new use classification and local market conditions
> - Upzoning (allowing denser or higher-value uses) tends to increase value; downzoning typically reduces it
> - Permit and land-use data from platforms like <a href="https://www.shovels.ai" target="_blank">Shovels</a> can help you research comparable rezonings and build a stronger application

## What Is Rezoning?

Rezoning is a formal change to the zoning designation of a <a href="https://www.shovels.ai/blog/what-is-a-parcel-real-estate/" target="_blank">parcel of land</a>. Every parcel in a jurisdiction that has adopted a zoning code is assigned a classification: residential, commercial, industrial, agricultural, mixed-use, or some subtype of these. That classification controls what can be built on the land, how the land can be used, and how densely it can be developed.

Rezoning changes that classification. A landowner, developer, or in some cases a local government itself can initiate a rezoning request. The change has to go through a formal approval process that typically includes a planning department review, a public hearing, and a vote by the local governing body.

It's worth distinguishing rezoning from two related concepts:

- **A variance** is an exception to existing zoning rules without changing the underlying classification. If your lot's setback requirement is 20 feet and you want to build 15 feet from the property line, you'd apply for a variance, not a rezone.
- **A special use permit** (sometimes called a conditional use permit) allows a specific use that isn't permitted by right under the current zoning, but without changing the designation itself. For example, a church in a residential zone might require a special use permit rather than a full rezone.

Rezoning, by contrast, changes the rules that apply to a parcel going forward, for all future owners and uses.

## What Does Rezoning Mean for Property Owners?

When a property is rezoned, its permitted uses change. Depending on the direction of the change, this can open up new development possibilities or constrain existing ones.

An upzone moves a property to a classification that allows denser or more intensive use, for example from single-family residential (R1) to multifamily residential (R3), or from residential to commercial. Upzones typically increase the range of what can be built and tend to increase land value.

A downzone moves a property to a more restrictive classification, for example from commercial to residential, or from a higher-density residential zone to single-family only. Downzones reduce what can be built on the land and typically decrease land value, sometimes significantly.

Rezoning also affects:

- **Property taxes**: A reclassification to commercial or industrial use typically results in a higher assessed value and higher tax burden
- **Financing**: Lenders evaluate collateral based on what's legally permissible on a parcel; a rezoned parcel may qualify for different (higher or lower) loan amounts
- **Neighbor relations**: Rezoning requests trigger public notice requirements and hearings, so neighboring property owners will have the opportunity to comment and object

## How Do You Rezone a Property?

The rezoning process is controlled by local government and varies by jurisdiction, but the general sequence is consistent across most counties and municipalities.

**1. Pre-application meeting.** Most planning departments strongly encourage (and some require) a pre-application meeting before you file. This is a chance to present your concept informally to planning staff, get feedback on whether your request is likely to be supported, and identify any issues to address in your application.

**2. Application filing.** You submit a formal rezoning application to the planning or zoning department. This typically includes a site plan, a statement of justification explaining why the rezoning is appropriate, documentation of how the request aligns with the jurisdiction's comprehensive plan, and applicable fees.

**3. Staff review.** Planning department staff review the application for completeness and then evaluate it against the jurisdiction's comprehensive plan, infrastructure capacity, compatibility with adjacent uses, and other criteria. Staff prepare a recommendation for the planning commission.

**4. Public notice.** Notice of the rezoning hearing is posted on the property and mailed to neighboring property owners within a defined radius (typically 300 to 500 feet, but this varies). This is when opposition, if any, begins to organize.

**5. Planning commission hearing.** The planning commission holds a public hearing, takes testimony from the applicant and the public, and makes a recommendation to approve, deny, or modify the request. In some jurisdictions, the planning commission's decision is final for certain types of rezonings. In others, it's advisory only.

**6. Governing body vote.** The city council, county board, or equivalent body holds a second public hearing and votes on the rezoning request. This is the final decision-making step.

**7. Appeal window.** After approval, there is typically a period during which opponents can appeal the decision to a board of zoning appeals or file a legal challenge. This window varies by jurisdiction but is commonly 30 to 60 days.

## How Long Does Rezoning Take and What Does It Cost?

Timelines vary considerably. A straightforward rezoning in a small jurisdiction with no opposition might move through in 60 to 90 days. A complex application in a large metro area, or any application that faces organized community opposition, can take one to three years or longer.

Typical cost components include:

- **Application fees:** These range from a few hundred dollars in smaller jurisdictions to $5,000–$20,000+ in major cities
- **Land-use attorney fees:** A land-use attorney is strongly recommended for any non-trivial rezoning; expect $5,000–$30,000+ depending on complexity and timeline
- **Traffic and environmental studies:** Many rezonings trigger requirements for traffic impact analyses, environmental assessments, or infrastructure studies; these can cost $10,000–$50,000+ each
- **Consultants and architects:** Site plans and renderings are typically required and may need to be prepared by licensed professionals

The total cost of a contested rezoning in an urban area, including professional fees and studies, can easily run six figures. For straightforward applications in smaller markets, total out-of-pocket costs might be $5,000–$15,000.

## Does Rezoning Increase Property Value?

Generally, yes, but it depends on the direction and nature of the change.

Upzoning to a higher-intensity use class almost always increases land value, because it expands what can be built. Converting a parcel from single-family residential to multifamily allows a developer to build more units. The additional density increases the land's value to a builder. Converting from residential to commercial can increase value dramatically if retail or office demand is strong in the area.

Research on upzoning effects is consistent: properties that are rezoned to allow higher-density residential or commercial use typically see land value increases ranging from 20% to 100%+ depending on the market, the specific change, and the development feasibility of the site.

Downzoning has the opposite effect. Studies of downzonings in suburban and agricultural areas have found land value decreases of 30% to 70% depending on how severely permitted uses are restricted.

A few factors that influence the magnitude of the value change:

- **Market demand:** Rezoning a commercial parcel in a market with no retail demand adds less value than the same rezone in a supply-constrained urban core
- **Infrastructure:** A parcel with sewer, water, and road access captures more value from a residential upzone than one that requires significant infrastructure investment
- **Entitlement risk:** The value of a site that *might* be rezoned is lower than the value of one that *has been* rezoned; entitlement risk is real and priced in

## Common Reasons Rezoning Applications Fail

Understanding why rezonings fail is as important as knowing how to file one.

**Inconsistency with the comprehensive plan.** Local governments adopt comprehensive plans (also called general plans or master plans) that set long-term land-use policy. A rezoning request that contradicts the comp plan starts with a significant disadvantage. Planning staff will note the inconsistency; planning commissioners and council members are likely to cite it as grounds for denial.

**Community opposition.** Organized opposition from neighboring property owners, neighborhood associations, or advocacy groups is one of the most common reasons rezonings fail or stall. Opposition doesn't automatically kill an application, but it raises political risk and often prompts elected officials to vote against approval.

**Infrastructure constraints.** If the rezoning would generate traffic, water, or sewer demand that existing infrastructure can't support, planning staff will flag it and the application may be conditioned on improvements, or denied outright.

**Inadequate application materials.** Rezoning applications that lack a strong statement of justification, don't address compatibility with adjacent uses, or fail to respond to staff comments tend to perform poorly at hearings.
